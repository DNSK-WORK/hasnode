In February 2026 I published *Looks Good to Me: On AI Sycophancy, Context Loss, and Inverted Baselines*.

It's about design review. More specifically, it's about why design review in product teams so reliably produces bad outcomes – and why AI made that problem worse before anyone had time to notice it was already a problem.

The title comes from a pattern I kept encountering. A designer shares work. The response is positive. The work ships. It fails. Retrospectively, everyone has notes. None of them were said out loud. "Looks good to me" is the three-word summary of a feedback culture that optimises for social comfort over useful signal – and it turns out AI tools fit that culture perfectly, because they are structurally incapable of the kind of friction that makes feedback useful.

The book is not a polemic against AI. It's an argument about the conditions under which feedback works, and why those conditions are more fragile than most teams assume. AI sycophancy is one part of it – the way generative tools default to agreement and elaboration rather than challenge. Context loss is another – what happens to design decisions when the reasoning behind them isn't carried forward and nobody remembers why the thing was built the way it was. Inverted baselines is the third – the slow process by which "acceptable" gets redefined downward when no one is keeping score.

I wrote it because I couldn't resolve the argument in a blog post. I tried. The [DNSK.WORK blog](https://dnsk.work/blog) has a series called [#Not\_My\_Type](https://dnsk.work/blog/category/not-my-type/) that is specifically for industry observations I find hard to be diplomatic about. I got partway through something on design review culture, and it kept expanding. At some point it became a book-length problem.
