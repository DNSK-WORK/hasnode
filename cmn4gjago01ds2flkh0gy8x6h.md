I'm 31, based in London, and I design the part of your product people complain about. Not the marketing site. The actual thing – the screen nobody wants to own, the flow that's been broken since v1, the internal tool your team uses every day while apologising for it. That's the work I find interesting.

My company is called [DNSK.WORK](https://dnsk.work). It's not an agency in the traditional sense – no account managers, no pitch decks, no six-week discovery phases that produce a strategy document and then hand you back to yourself. I work as an embedded UX/UI design partner, which means I sit inside product teams the way a senior designer would, except I'm not on your payroll and I'm not waiting for a promotion. The model exists because most scaling teams don't need a full design department – they need one person who can move fast, think structurally, and hand off clean work. That's what DNSK.WORK does.

I've been doing this long enough to have worked across enough products to notice patterns. Fintech. SaaS platforms. Internal tools. Enterprise dashboards. The problems repeat more than people expect. The solutions are usually less complicated than the teams involved would prefer.

In February 2026 I wrote and published [*Looks Good to Me: On AI Sycophancy, Context Loss, and Inverted Baselines*](https://play.google.com/store/books/details?id=s8vBEQAAQBAJ) – a book about design review, feedback culture, and why most design criticism in product teams produces worse outcomes than saying nothing. Also on [Apple Books](https://books.apple.com/us/book/looks-good-to-me-on-ai-sycophancy-context-loss-and/id6760121233). It came out of a specific frustration I couldn't resolve in a blog post.

Speaking of which – I run a [blog at DNSK.WORK](https://dnsk.work/blog) where I write about design work in two registers. One is tactical: case studies, methods, what actually worked on a real project. The other is a series called [#Not\_My\_Type](https://dnsk.work/blog/category/not-my-type/), which is more honest about how the industry actually functions. I also publish occasionally at BLOG@CACM – the ACM's practitioner blog – on topics that need a more technically rigorous home.

I'm an Awwwards Judge, which means I've looked at a lot of websites in a professional capacity and have opinions about most of them.

This Hashnode blog is for everything that doesn't fit neatly into any of the above. Notes on work. Observations about tools. Things I noticed on a project that aren't long enough for the blog or formal enough for CACM. Occasionally something personal. The name says it: on work and other things.

If any of this is relevant to something you're building, DNSK.WORK is at dnsk.work. The blog is at dnsk.work/blog. The book is findable.
