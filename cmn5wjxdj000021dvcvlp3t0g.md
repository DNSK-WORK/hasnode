It didn't arrive with a banner. No moment where I decided to "adopt AI into my workflow." It crept in through tools I was already using – background removal that got smarter, copy suggestions that appeared uninvited, Figma plugins that started offering layout recommendations.

For a while it was genuinely useful. Then something shifted.

I started noticing that the output was getting worse. Not dramatically, not in a way that was easy to flag in a meeting – just subtly, consistently worse. The same tools I'd relied on for two years started producing results that looked right but weren't quite. I wrote about it properly in [AI model collapse and design tools](https://dnsk.work/blog/ai-model-collapse-design-tools/) – the short version is that when AI tools train on AI-generated output, quality degrades in ways that are hard to measure until you go back and compare.

That was the first problem. The second was more interesting.

AI is very good at agreeing with you. It's good at completing your thought, extending your direction, elaborating on whatever you've already started. What it's not good at is telling you that the direction is wrong. Not because it can't – but because it's optimised for helpfulness, and helpfulness, as it turns out, looks a lot like agreement.

I'd seen this pattern in design review long before AI entered the picture. [Silence in design feedback](https://dnsk.work/blog/silence-feedback/) isn't neutral – it reads as approval, and then six weeks later there's a churn problem nobody can explain. AI didn't invent that dynamic. It just automated it and scaled it up.

That's more or less where the book came from. *Looks Good to Me: On AI Sycophancy, Context Loss, and Inverted Baselines* is my attempt to work through what happens to design quality when the feedback loop is optimised for comfort rather than accuracy – and what AI specifically does to make that worse.

I'm a designer. I work with SaaS product teams at [DNSK.WORK](https://dnsk.work). I'm not a researcher or an AI critic – I'm someone who uses these tools every day and started keeping notes on what was actually happening.
